Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to build a SaaS from scratch?

The total Year 1 cost of building a SaaS product from scratch typically ranges from $5,000 to $50,000+ depending on your tech stack, developer hourly rate, and infrastructure choices. This includes 150–300+ hours of development for core features like authentication, payments, database setup, and a landing page, plus $50–$300/month in hosting and service costs.

How long does it take to build a SaaS product?

Building a SaaS from scratch typically takes 150–300+ development hours. This breaks down to: authentication (40–60 hours), payment integration (30–50 hours), database and ORM setup (10–20 hours), dashboard UI (15–30 hours), landing page (20–40 hours), and middleware (4–8 hours). Using a starter kit can reduce this to 20–40 hours total.

What are the monthly infrastructure costs for a SaaS?

Monthly infrastructure costs typically range from $0 (free tiers) to $300+/month. Common costs include hosting ($0–200/month on Vercel, AWS, or Railway), managed database ($5–50/month for PostgreSQL or Supabase), and auth services ($0–25/month for Clerk or Supabase Auth). Most early-stage SaaS products run on $20–50/month.

Is it cheaper to use a boilerplate or build from scratch?

Using a boilerplate is significantly cheaper. Building from scratch at $75/hour costs $11,000–$22,000+ in development alone. A starter kit like LaunchFast ($79 one-time) includes auth, payments, database, AI, and more — saving 80–200+ hours. The breakeven happens within the first few hours of saved development work.

What tech stack should I use for my SaaS in 2026?

The most popular SaaS tech stack in 2026 includes Next.js for the frontend, PostgreSQL with Prisma ORM for the database, NextAuth or Clerk for authentication, Stripe for payments, and Vercel or Railway for hosting. This stack balances developer experience, scalability, and cost-effectiveness well.

How can I reduce SaaS development costs?

The most effective ways: 1) Use a SaaS boilerplate to skip 80+ hours of setup, 2) Start with managed services (Supabase, Clerk) instead of building from scratch, 3) Use free tiers during early stages, 4) Focus on an MVP with core features only, and 5) Choose a tech stack with strong ecosystem support to reduce debugging time.
